    Determinants

    Introduction

    The determinant is a function which receives as input a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$ and yields, as output, a number denoted by $\det(\Mtrx{A})$. We are interested in determinants because:

    1. determinants enable us to develop the concept of `oriented volume' in $n$-dimensional space, and

    2. determinants provide a new test for invertibility of a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$: $\Mtrx{A}$ is invertible exactly when $\det(\Mtrx{A})\neq 0$.

    Let us preview our determinant topics in some more detail.

    A preview of how to compute determinants

    Preview: Computing Determinants

    To define a quantity $Q$ depending on a number $n$ of inputs recursively means:

    1. First define $Q$ for a single input.

    2. Using this information, define $Q$ for two inputs.

    3. Using knowledge of $Q$ for two inputs, define $Q$ for three inputs.

    4. etc.

    Determinants of square matrices are defined recursively as follows:

    The determinant of a $(1,1)$-matrix $\Mtrx{A} = [x]$ is

    $$\det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det [x] := x$$

    The determinant of a $(2,2)$-matrix

    $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \end{array} \right] $$

    is given by

    $$ \det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 \end{array} \right] := {\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 $$

    The recursive nature of this definition becomes more explicit if we write

    $${\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 = {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det[ y_2 ] - {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det [y_1]$$

    The determinant of a $(3,3)$-matrix

    $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] $$

    is given by

    $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &= \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] \\ % &:= {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_2 & z_2 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ -\ {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ +\ {\color{red} x_3}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_2 & z_2 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

    Now suppose the determinant of matrices of size $(n,n)$ has been defined. Then the determinant of an $(n+1,n+1)$-matrix

    $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} {\color{red} a_{1,1}} & a_{1,2} & \cdots & a_{1,n+1} \\ {\color{red} a_{2,1}} & a_{2,2} & \cdots & a_{2,n+1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}} & a_{n+1,2} & \cdots & a_{n+1,n+1} \\ \end{array} \right] $$

    is given by

    $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &:= {\color{red} a_{1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{1,1}) - {\color{red} a_{2,1}}\cdot \det(A_{2,1}) + - \cdots \\ &\qquad \cdots + (-1)^{(n+1)+1} {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{n+1,1}) \endaligned $$

    In this formula $A_{i,1}$ is the $(n,n)$-matrix which results from $\Mtrx{A}$ by removing the $i$-th row and the first column.

    As you can see, this recursive formula for computing a determinant is not difficult in principle. However, the number of computational steps involved increases rapidly with the size of $\Mtrx{A}$.

    A preview of how to compute with determinants

    Preview: Rules for Computing with Determinants

    The following rules for computing with determinants lead to an interpretation of determinants as `oriented volume' and, in addition, can often simplify the process of evaluating a determinant

    Multilinear When applying the determinant operation to $(n,n)$-matrices, varying one column vector, while keeping the others fixed, yields a linear function $\RNr{n} \to \RNr{}$. For example:

    $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} + {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} + {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} + {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ &=\ {\color{red} 3}\cdot \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 1} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 2} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

    Alternating   Interchanging any two columns of a matrix reverses the sign of its determinant. For example:

    $$ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = -\det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] = 1 $$

    Additional rules

    • $\det(\Mtrx{A}^T) = \det(\Mtrx{A})$

    • $\det(\Mtrx{A}\cdot \Mtrx{B}) = \det(\Mtrx{A}) \det(\Mtrx{B})$

    A preview of ‘orientation’

    Preview: Orientation

    We introduce the concept of orientation of $\RNr{n}$ as a means for distinguishing an object in $\RNr{n}$ from its mirrored image. In this preview we only discuss the visualizable dimensions 1,2,3.

    1-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{}$ is given by a nonzero vector $\Vect{x} = (x)$. We say

    $\Vect{x}$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{}$

    if $ x> 0 $

    $\omega(\Vect{x}) = +1$

    $\Vect{x}$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{}$

    if $ x < 0 $

    $\omega(\Vect{x}) = -1$

    `noncolinear' means: don't lie on one line; in other words: point in distinct directions

    2-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{2}$ is given by a pair of noncolinear vectors $(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$. We say

    $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{2}$

    if the lesser of the two angles from $\Vect{x}$ to $\Vect{y}$ occurs in the counterclockwise direction

    $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} ) = +1$

    $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{2}$

    if the lesser of the two angles from $\Vect{x}$ to $\Vect{y}$ occurs in the clockwise direction

    $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} ) = -1$

    `noncoplanar' means: don't lie in one plane

    3-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{3}$ is given by a triple of noncoplanar vectors $(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$. We say

    $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{3}$

    if it is possible to align your right hand's thumb with $\Vect{x}$, its index finger with $\Vect{y}$, and its middle finger with $\Vect{z}$.

    without harming your hand

    $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} ) = +1$

    $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{3}$

    if it is possible to align your left hand's thumb with $\Vect{x}$, its index finger with $\Vect{y}$, and its middle finger with $\Vect{z}$.

    without harming your hand

    $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} ) = -1$

    We also explain the relationship between orientation of 3-space and rotation about an oriented axis according to the right/left hand rule.

    A preview of ‘oriented volume’

    Preview: Oriented Volume

    The central result of this section is the following geometrical interpretation of the determinant operation.

    Theorem – Let $\Vect{x}_1, \dots , \Vect{x}_n$ be $n$ vectors in $\RNr{n}$, and write them as column vectors. Then

    $$\det [ \Vect{x}_1\ \dots\ \Vect{x}_n] = \omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n ) \cdot \Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$$

    In the formula above, $\omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the orientation of $\RNr{n}$ represented by the $n$-tuple of vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$. This is $+1$ if the orientation is positive, and it is $-1$ if the orientation is negative. Finally, $\Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the volume of the slanted box in $\RNr{n}$ spanned by the vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$.

    Already the following two examples illustrate the simple beauty of this result.

    Example – the result

    $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rr} 4320 & 4310 \\ 89 & 87 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 4320\cdot 87\ -\ 4310\cdot 89 \\ &=\ 375840\ -\ 383590 \\ &<\ 0 \endaligned $$

    means

    • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (4320,89)$ and $\Vect{y} = (4310,87)$ span a parallelogram whose area is $\Abs{375840 - 383590} = 383590 - 375840$.

    • The pair of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the negative or clockwise orientation of the plane.

    Example – the result

    $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 3 & 7 & -1 \\ -2 & 4 & 3 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 72 + 21 - (-4 + (-84)) \\ &=\ 181 > 0 \endaligned $$

    means

    • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (3,-2,1)$, $\Vect{y} = (7,4,0)$, $\Vect{z} = (-1,3,6)$ span a slanted box in $\RNr{3}$ whose volume is $181$.

    • The ordered triple of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive or right hand orientation of the $\RNr{3}$.

    A preview of ‘cross product’

    Preview: Cross Product

    The cross product of two vectors $\Vect{x} = (x_1,x_2,x_3)$ and $\Vect{y} = (y_1,y_2,y_3)$ in $\RNr{3}$ is the vector in $\RNr{3}$ given by the formula

    $$ \aligned \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }\ &=\ \left( \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_2 & y_2 \\ x_3 & y_3 \end{array} \right] , -\left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_3 & y_3 \end{array} \right] , \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \end{array} \right] \right) \\ % &=\ \left( x_2y_3-x_3y_2 , -(x_1y_3-x_3y_1) , x_1y_2-x_2y_1 \right) \endaligned $$

    Below we formulate the algebraic properties and the geometric properties of the cross product, as well as its relationship to the dot product and the determinant.

    For deeper reasons, the cross product of two vectors is only available in $\RNr{3}$ and in $\RNr{7}$.

    Algebraic properties of the cross product

    • bilinear

      $\CrssPr{ (\Vect{x} + \Vect{x}' )}{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x}' }{ \Vect{y} }$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ (\Vect{y} + \Vect{y}') }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y}' }$ $\CrssPr{ (t\cdot \Vect{x}) }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $t\cdot ( \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } )$

    • antisymmetric

      $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $-\left( \CrssPr{ \Vect{y} }{ \Vect{x} } \right)$

    Geometric properties of the cross product

    • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ is perpendicular to both $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

    • If $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } \neq \Vect{0}$, then the vector triple $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })$ represents the right hand orientation of $\RNr{3}$.

    • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } = \Vect{0}$ if and only if $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ are parallel.

    • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

    • If $\Vect{x},\Vect{y} \neq \Vect{0}$ then $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } } = \abs{ \Vect{x} } \abs{ \Vect{y} }\cdot \sin \sphericalangle( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$.

    • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }^2 = \abs{ \Vect{x} }^2 \abs{ \Vect{y} }^2 - ( \DotPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })^2$

    • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{a} }{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) } = (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{y} } )\cdot \Vect{x}\ -\ (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{x} }) \cdot \Vect{y}$.

    Cross product, dot product and determinant

    $$\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) } = \det[ \Vect{a}\ \ \Vect{x} \ \ \Vect{y} ]$$

    1. How to compute determinants   We first define the determinant of $(1,1)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(2,2)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(3,3)$-matrices and so on. – A few experiments will show you that, with increasing size of matrices, the number of computations one has to carry out increases extremely rapidly.

    2. Rules for computing with determinants – Given that computing determinants of large matrices requires a lot of computations, it is doubly important that there are rules for computing with determinants which are quick and efficient. Some of these rules may seem counter intuitive at first. However, we will explain how these rules correspond perfectly to the properties of the volume function on boxes.

      In addition, these rules will guide us to an alternate method of computing determinants using row and column operations. For determinants of large matrices this method is much more efficient than the recursive one we discussed earlier.

    3. Orientation of space – Each $\RNr{n}$ carries two distinct orientations. This is a subtle property of space. In $\RNr{3}$ we encounter it by considering our two hands. While our hands are mirrored images of one another, they are not interchangeable, that is: we cannot replace a left hand by a right hand. Thus we need to distinguish carefully between ‘left’ and ‘right’. We encounter the need for such a distinction in each $\RNr{n}$, and the determinant operation will help us make such a distinction.

    4. Oriented volume – Here we combine what we learned about properties of the determinant operation with properties of the volume of boxes and orientation. The result is the surprisingly powerful notion of oriented volume, computed using determinants.

    5. Cross product – operation is only defined for pairs of vectors in $\RNr{3}$. Given two vectors $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ from $\RNr{3}$, it returns the vector $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ of $\RNr{3}$, called the cross product of $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$. The geometric properties of the cross product operation make it a very powerful tool in mechanical engineering and in parts of physics.

    6. Cramer's rule – if a system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ variables has an invertible coefficient matrix, then we can express the unique solution of this system by a formula which uses the determinant operation.

    Some historic highlights

    • The history of determinants started with an analysis of the number of solutions of a system of linear equations. The determinant of the unaugmented coefficient matrix of a homogeneous system of $n$ linear equations is nonzero exactly when the system has the unique solution $(0,\dots ,0)$.

    • The term ‘determinant’ appeared first in C. F. Gauß’s work on quadratic forms to express the fact that the determinant determines the properties of the quadratic form. However, the concept to which Gauß applied the term ‘determinant’ is distinct from the concept which we call ‘determinant’ today.

    • The current use of the term ‘determinant’ goes back at least as far as A.-L. Cauchy . He is credited with publishing the most complete of the early works on determinants in 1812 .

    • Later Weierstrass used an axiomatic definition of ‘determinant’. This means he used properties like ‘multilinear’, ‘alternating’, to define the determinant.

    For vectors $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ in $\RNr{n}$, the following hold:

    1. Relationship between dot product and norm

    2. Orthogonality criterion

      $\DotPr{\Vect{x}}{\Vect{y}} = 0$ if and only if $\Vect{x}$ is perpendicular to $\Vect{y}$.

      $\Abs{\Vect{x} + \Vect{y}} \leq \Abs{\Vect{x}} + \Abs{\Vect{y}}$

      $| \DotPr{\Vect{x}}{\Vect{y}} | \leq | \Vect{x} | | \Vect{y} |$.

    The Coefficient Matrix of a System of Linear Equations

    Introduction

    RREF = Row Reduced Echelon Form

    We are now going to learn how to work with systems of linear equations more economically. Observe that, when reducing a system of linear equations to RREF , we really only manipulate its coefficients, not its variables. So, by carrying the variables along all the time we just do a lot of unnecessary work. – How can we do better?

    We save ourselves a lot of work by extracting from the system its coefficients and writing them in a rectangle whose rows and columns resemble the original system. Thus we obtain the (augmented) coefficient matrix of the given system of linear equations. Now the operations on equations in a system translate into operations on rows in the associated augmented coefficient matrix. The details follow.

    Example

    Find the solutions of the system of linear equations below by extracting its augmented coefficient matrix and transforming it to RREF.

    $$ \begin{array}{rcrcrcrcr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}z} & = & 1 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-9$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$6$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$}{\color{red}z} & = & 9 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$}{\color{red}z} & = & 14 \\ \end{array} $$



    Solution

    The augmented coefficient matrix of the given system of linear equations is.

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|r} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-9$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$6$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & 9 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$} & 14 \end{array} $$

    The part on the left of the vertical bar is the coefficient matrix. The part on the right is the augmented part, also called the augmentation vector. To find the solutions of the given system of linear equations, we reduce the augmented coefficient matrix to RREF. We begin by interchanging the first two rows:

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-9$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$6$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & 9 & {\color{red} :3} \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$} & 14 & \end{array} $$

    Dividing the first row by $3$ yields

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$} & 14 & {\color{red} -3\cdot R_1} \end{array} $$

    Subtracting $3$ times the first row from the third yields

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$10$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-5$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5$} & 5 & {\color{red} (-5)\cdot R_2} \end{array} $$

    Subtracting $5$ times the second row from the third yields

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & {\color{red} :2}\\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

    Dividing the second row by $2$ yields

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & {\color{red} +3\cdot R_2 }\\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & 1/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

    Adding $3$ times row 2 to row $1$ yields.

    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5/2$} & 9/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & 1/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

    The matrix above is in Row Reduced Echelon Form. This tells us that the original system of linear equations is equivalent to the system.

    $$ \begin{array}{rcrcrcrcr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 9/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 1/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}z} & = & 0 \\ \end{array} $$

    Thus we see that its solutions are obtained by choosing freely number values for $y$ and $z$ and then solving for the variables tied to leading $1$:

    $$ \aligned x\ &=\ \dfrac{1}{2}\ +\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, z \\ w\ &=\ \dfrac{9}{2}\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{5}{2}\, z \endaligned $$

    Consequently, the solutions of this system of linear equations are vectors of the form

    $$ \begin{array}{l} \left( \dfrac{9}{2}\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{5}{2}\, z , \dfrac{1}{2}\, +\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, z , y , z \right) = \\ \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot (9,1,0,0) + y\cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0) + z\cdot (-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1) \end{array} $$

    where $y$ and $z$ are arbitrary real numbers. So we see that the solutions of the given system of linear equations are spanned by the vectors $(-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0)$ and $(-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1)$ and is shifted by the vector $(\tfrac{9}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},0,0)$.



    Thus we can save ourselves quite a bit of writing effort by working with the augmented coefficient matrix instead of the full system of linear equations.

      Determinants

      Introduction

      The determinant is a function which receives as input a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$ and yields, as output, a number denoted by $\det(\Mtrx{A})$. We are interested in determinants because:

      1. determinants enable us to develop the concept of `oriented volume' in $n$-dimensional space, and

      2. determinants provide a new test for invertibility of a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$: $\Mtrx{A}$ is invertible exactly when $\det(\Mtrx{A})\neq 0$.

      Let us preview our determinant topics in some more detail.

      A preview of how to compute determinants

      Preview: Computing Determinants

      To define a quantity $Q$ depending on a number $n$ of inputs recursively means:

      1. First define $Q$ for a single input.

      2. Using this information, define $Q$ for two inputs.

      3. Using knowledge of $Q$ for two inputs, define $Q$ for three inputs.

      4. etc.

      Determinants of square matrices are defined recursively as follows:

      The determinant of a $(1,1)$-matrix $\Mtrx{A} = [x]$ is

      $$\det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det [x] := x$$

      The determinant of a $(2,2)$-matrix

      $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \end{array} \right] $$

      is given by

      $$ \det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 \end{array} \right] := {\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 $$

      The recursive nature of this definition becomes more explicit if we write

      $${\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 = {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det[ y_2 ] - {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det [y_1]$$

      The determinant of a $(3,3)$-matrix

      $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] $$

      is given by

      $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &= \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] \\ % &:= {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_2 & z_2 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ -\ {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ +\ {\color{red} x_3}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_2 & z_2 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

      Now suppose the determinant of matrices of size $(n,n)$ has been defined. Then the determinant of an $(n+1,n+1)$-matrix

      $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} {\color{red} a_{1,1}} & a_{1,2} & \cdots & a_{1,n+1} \\ {\color{red} a_{2,1}} & a_{2,2} & \cdots & a_{2,n+1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}} & a_{n+1,2} & \cdots & a_{n+1,n+1} \\ \end{array} \right] $$

      is given by

      $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &:= {\color{red} a_{1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{1,1}) - {\color{red} a_{2,1}}\cdot \det(A_{2,1}) + - \cdots \\ &\qquad \cdots + (-1)^{(n+1)+1} {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{n+1,1}) \endaligned $$

      In this formula $A_{i,1}$ is the $(n,n)$-matrix which results from $\Mtrx{A}$ by removing the $i$-th row and the first column.

      As you can see, this recursive formula for computing a determinant is not difficult in principle. However, the number of computational steps involved increases rapidly with the size of $\Mtrx{A}$.

      Preview: Rules for Computing with Determinants

      The following rules for computing with determinants lead to an interpretation of determinants as `oriented volume' and, in addition, can often simplify the process of evaluating a determinant

      Multilinear When applying the determinant operation to $(n,n)$-matrices, varying one column vector, while keeping the others fixed, yields a linear function $\RNr{n} \to \RNr{}$. For example:

      $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} + {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} + {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} + {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ &=\ {\color{red} 3}\cdot \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 1} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 2} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

      $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{3}$

      without harming your hand

      A preview of ‘oriented volume’

      Preview: Oriented Volume

      The central result of this section is the following geometrical interpretation of the determinant operation.

      Theorem – Let $\Vect{x}_1, \dots , \Vect{x}_n$ be $n$ vectors in $\RNr{n}$, and write them as column vectors. Then

      $$\det [ \Vect{x}_1\ \dots\ \Vect{x}_n] = \omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n ) \cdot \Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$$

      In the formula above, $\omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the orientation of $\RNr{n}$ represented by the $n$-tuple of vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$. This is $+1$ if the orientation is positive, and it is $-1$ if the orientation is negative. Finally, $\Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the volume of the slanted box in $\RNr{n}$ spanned by the vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$.

      Already the following two examples illustrate the simple beauty of this result.

      Example – the result

      $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rr} 4320 & 4310 \\ 89 & 87 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 4320\cdot 87\ -\ 4310\cdot 89 \\ &=\ 375840\ -\ 383590 \\ &<\ 0 \endaligned $$

      means

      • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (4320,89)$ and $\Vect{y} = (4310,87)$ span a parallelogram whose area is $\Abs{375840 - 383590} = 383590 - 375840$.

      • The pair of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the negative or clockwise orientation of the plane.

      Example – the result

      $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 3 & 7 & -1 \\ -2 & 4 & 3 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 72 + 21 - (-4 + (-84)) \\ &=\ 181 > 0 \endaligned $$

      means

      • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (3,-2,1)$, $\Vect{y} = (7,4,0)$, $\Vect{z} = (-1,3,6)$ span a slanted box in $\RNr{3}$ whose volume is $181$.

      • The ordered triple of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive or right hand orientation of the $\RNr{3}$.

      Below we formulate the algebraic properties and the geometric properties of the cross product, as well as its relationship to the dot product and the determinant.

      For deeper reasons, the cross product of two vectors is only available in $\RNr{3}$ and in $\RNr{7}$.

      Algebraic properties of the cross product

      • bilinear

        $\CrssPr{ (\Vect{x} + \Vect{x}' )}{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x}' }{ \Vect{y} }$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ (\Vect{y} + \Vect{y}') }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y}' }$ $\CrssPr{ (t\cdot \Vect{x}) }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $t\cdot ( \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } )$

      • antisymmetric

        $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $-\left( \CrssPr{ \Vect{y} }{ \Vect{x} } \right)$

      Geometric properties of the cross product

      • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ is perpendicular to both $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

      • If $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } \neq \Vect{0}$, then the vector triple $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })$ represents the right hand orientation of $\RNr{3}$.

      • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } = \Vect{0}$ if and only if $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ are parallel.

      • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

      • If $\Vect{x},\Vect{y} \neq \Vect{0}$ then $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } } = \abs{ \Vect{x} } \abs{ \Vect{y} }\cdot \sin \sphericalangle( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$.

      • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }^2 = \abs{ \Vect{x} }^2 \abs{ \Vect{y} }^2 - ( \DotPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })^2$

      • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{a} }{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) } = (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{y} } )\cdot \Vect{x}\ -\ (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{x} }) \cdot \Vect{y}$.

      1. How to compute determinants   We first define the determinant of $(1,1)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(2,2)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(3,3)$-matrices and so on. – A few experiments will show you that, with increasing size of matrices, the number of computations one has to carry out increases extremely rapidly.

      2. Rules for computing with determinants – Given that computing determinants of large matrices requires a lot of computations, it is doubly important that there are rules for computing with determinants which are quick and efficient. Some of these rules may seem counter intuitive at first. However, we will explain how these rules correspond perfectly to the properties of the volume function on boxes.

        In addition, these rules will guide us to an alternate method of computing determinants using row and column operations. For determinants of large matrices this method is much more efficient than the recursive one we discussed earlier.

      3. Orientation of space – Each $\RNr{n}$ carries two distinct orientations. This is a subtle property of space. In $\RNr{3}$ we encounter it by considering our two hands. While our hands are mirrored images of one another, they are not interchangeable, that is: we cannot replace a left hand by a right hand. Thus we need to distinguish carefully between ‘left’ and ‘right’. We encounter the need for such a distinction in each $\RNr{n}$, and the determinant operation will help us make such a distinction.

      4. Oriented volume – Here we combine what we learned about properties of the determinant operation with properties of the volume of boxes and orientation. The result is the surprisingly powerful notion of oriented volume, computed using determinants.

      5. Cross product – operation is only defined for pairs of vectors in $\RNr{3}$. Given two vectors $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ from $\RNr{3}$, it returns the vector $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ of $\RNr{3}$, called the cross product of $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$. The geometric properties of the cross product operation make it a very powerful tool in mechanical engineering and in parts of physics.

      6. Cramer's rule – if a system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ variables has an invertible coefficient matrix, then we can express the unique solution of this system by a formula which uses the determinant operation.

      • The history of determinants started with an analysis of the number of solutions of a system of linear equations. The determinant of the unaugmented coefficient matrix of a homogeneous system of $n$ linear equations is nonzero exactly when the system has the unique solution $(0,\dots ,0)$.

      • The term ‘determinant’ appeared first in C. F. Gauß’s work on quadratic forms to express the fact that the determinant determines the properties of the quadratic form.       • The current use of the term ‘determinant’ goes back at least as far as A.-L. Cauchy . He is credited with publishing the most complete of the early works on determinants in 1812 .

      Introduction

      RREF = Row Reduced Echelon Form

      We are now going to learn how to work with systems of linear equations more economically. Observe that, when reducing a system of linear equations to RREF , we really only manipulate its coefficients, not its variables. So, by carrying the variables along all the time we just do a lot of unnecessary work. – How can we do better?

        We use the term matrix to refer to any rectangular arrangement of objects. Here, in linear algebra, we encounter primarily matrices of numbers, that is rectangular arrangement of numbers . Such an arrangement can serve any one of a vast variety of organizational purposes. For example, when we learned how to solve system of linear equations we arranged the coefficients of the system in a matrix to facilitate the execution of row operations.

        But matrices can do much more than serve organizational purposes: like vectors, matrices of equal size can be added; it is possible to multiply a matrix by a number; and then there is a new operation: whenever the sizes of two matrices $\Mtrx{A}$ and $\Mtrx{B}$ are compatible, the product $\Mtrx{A}\cdot \Mtrx{B}$ is defined.

        Cross Products in Arbitrary Dimensions?

        The operations ‘dot product’ and ‘determinant’ are defined for all dimensions $n\geq 1$. Analogously we would expect a definition of the cross product in dimensions $n\geq 1$. There are two interpretations

        • We look for an alternating and multilinear operation which assigns to an ordered $(n-1)$-tuple of vectors $(\Vect{b}_1,\dots ,\Vect{b}_{n-1})$ in $\RNr{n}$ a vector $\text{Cross}(\Vect{b}_1,\dots ,\Vect{b}_{n-1})$ in $\RNr{n}$ such that

          $\det[\Vect{b}_1\ \ \cdots\ \ \Vect{b}_{n-1}\ \ \text{Cross}(\Vect{b}_1,\dots ,\Vect{b}_{n-1})]$ $ > $ $0$

          $(\Vect{b}_1,\dots ,\Vect{b}_{n-1})$ is linearly independent. Such an operation exists, but is usually not referred to as a ‘cross product’.

        • We look for an alternating bilinear operation on ordered pairs of vectors $(\Vect{u},\Vect{v})$ such that $\CrssPr{ \Vect{u} }{ \Vect{v} }\neq \Vect{0}$ whenever $\Vect{u}$ and $\Vect{v}$ are not parallel, and such that $\CrssPr{ \Vect{u} }{ \Vect{v} }$ is perpendicular to both $\Vect{u}$ and $\Vect{v}$.

          For deeper reasons, such an operation is only possible if $n=3$ or $n=7$.

        Advanced information   We mention two results on the (non-)existence of cross products of pairs. First a now classical result from algebraic topology:

        Theorem   Suppose $\RNr{n}$ admits a cross product operation satisfying

        • If $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ are not parallel, then $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }\neq 0$.

        • For all $\Vect{x},\Vect{y}\in \RNr{n}$, $\DotPr{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) }{ \Vect{x} }\neq 0$ and $\DotPr{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) }{ \Vect{y} }\neq 0$

        • $\CrssPr{-}{-}\from \RNr{n}\times \RNr{n} \longrightarrow \RNr{n}$ is a continuous function

        Then $n=3$ or $n=7$.

        If we add to the list of assumptions above the requirement that the cross product be bilinear, then the conclusion of the theorem can be proven by elementary algebraic means. This is a classical theorem of Frobenius.

            $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & {\color{red} +3\cdot R_2 }\\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & 1/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

            Adding $3$ times row 2 to row $1$ yields.

            $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5/2$} & 9/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & 1/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

            The matrix above is in Row Reduced Echelon Form. This tells us that the original system of linear equations is equivalent to the system.

            $$ \begin{array}{rcrcrcrcr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 9/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 1/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}z} & = & 0 \\ \end{array} $$

            Thus we see that its solutions are obtained by choosing freely number values for $y$ and $z$ and then solving for the variables tied to leading $1$:

            $$ \aligned x\ &=\ \dfrac{1}{2}\ +\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, z \\ w\ &=\ \dfrac{9}{2}\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{5}{2}\, z \endaligned $$

            Consequently, the solutions of this system of linear equations are vectors of the form

            $$ \begin{array}{l} \left( \dfrac{9}{2}\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{5}{2}\, z , \dfrac{1}{2}\, +\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, z , y , z \right) = \\ \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot (9,1,0,0) + y\cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0) + z\cdot (-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1) \end{array} $$

            where $y$ and $z$ are arbitrary real numbers. So we see that the solutions of the given system of linear equations are spanned by the vectors $(-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0)$ and $(-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1)$ and is shifted by the vector $(\tfrac{9}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},0,0)$.



            Thus we can save ourselves quite a bit of writing effort by working with the augmented coefficient matrix instead of the full system of linear equations.

              We use the term matrix to refer to any rectangular arrangement of objects. Here, in linear algebra, we encounter primarily matrices of numbers, that is rectangular arrangement of numbers . Such an arrangement can serve any one of a vast variety of organizational purposes. For example, when we learned how to solve system of linear equations we arranged the coefficients of the system in a matrix to facilitate the execution of row operations.

              But matrices can do much more than serve organizational purposes: like vectors, matrices of equal size can be added; it is possible to multiply a matrix by a number; and then there is a new operation: whenever the sizes of two matrices $\Mtrx{A}$ and $\Mtrx{B}$ are compatible, the product $\Mtrx{A}\cdot \Mtrx{B}$ is defined.

              It is possible to embed the system $\RNr{}$ of real numbers in the system of matrices, for example by turning a number $x$ into the matrix $[x]$ consisting of a single entry. Thus the system of all matrices vastly extends the system of real numbers, an extension which is enormously versatile and powerful.

              We will use matrices for a variety of purposes: they will enable us to obtain additional tools to solve systems of linear equations. Further, we will learn how to use matrices to transform space, how to manipulate the location of objects in space, and how alter the shape of objects in space.

              RREF = Row Reduced Echelon Form

              We are now going to learn how to work with systems of linear equations more economically. Observe that, when reducing a system of linear equations to RREF , we really only manipulate its coefficients, not its variables. So, by carrying the variables along all the time we just do a lot of unnecessary work. – How can we do better?

                    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-9$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$6$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & 9 & {\color{red} :3} \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$} & 14 & \end{array} $$

                    Dividing the first row by $3$ yields

                    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$8$} & 14 & {\color{red} -3\cdot R_1} \end{array} $$

                    Subtracting $3$ times the first row from the third yields

                    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 1 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$10$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-5$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5$} & 5 & {\color{red} (-5)\cdot R_2} \end{array} $$

                    $$ \begin{array}{rrrr|rr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-3$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & 3 & {\color{red} +3\cdot R_2 }\\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$} & 1/2 & \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$} & 0 & \end{array} $$

                    The matrix above is in Row Reduced Echelon Form. This tells us that the original system of linear equations is equivalent to the system.

                    $$ \begin{array}{rcrcrcrcr} \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$5/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 9/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$-1/2$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$1/2$}{\color{red}z} & = & 1/2 \\ % \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}w} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}x} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}y} & + & \colorbox{lightgreen}{$0$}{\color{red}z} & = & 0 \\ \end{array} $$

                    Thus we see that its solutions are obtained by choosing freely number values for $y$ and $z$ and then solving for the variables tied to leading $1$:

                    $$ \aligned x\ &=\ \dfrac{1}{2}\ +\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, z \\ w\ &=\ \dfrac{9}{2}\ -\ \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\ -\ \dfrac{5}{2}\, z \endaligned $$

                    Consequently, the solutions of this system of linear equations are vectors of the form

                    $$ \begin{array}{l} \left( \dfrac{9}{2}\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{5}{2}\, z , \dfrac{1}{2}\, +\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, z , y , z \right) = \\ \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot (9,1,0,0) + y\cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0) + z\cdot (-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1) \end{array} $$

                    where $y$ and $z$ are arbitrary real numbers. So we see that the solutions of the given system of linear equations are spanned by the vectors $(-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0)$ and $(-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1)$ and is shifted by the vector $(\tfrac{9}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},0,0)$.



                    Thus we can save ourselves quite a bit of writing effort by working with the augmented coefficient matrix instead of the full system of linear equations.

                      RankDefinition

                      We use the term matrix to refer to any rectangular arrangement of objects. Here, in linear algebra, we encounter primarily matrices of numbers, that is rectangular arrangement of numbers . Such an arrangement can serve any one of a vast variety of organizational purposes. For example, when we learned how to solve system of linear equations we arranged the coefficients of the system in a matrix to facilitate the execution of row operations.

                      But matrices can do much more than serve organizational purposes: like vectors, matrices of equal size can be added; it is possible to multiply a matrix by a number; and then there is a new operation: whenever the sizes of two matrices $\Mtrx{A}$ and $\Mtrx{B}$ are compatible, the product $\Mtrx{A}\cdot \Mtrx{B}$ is defined.

                      It is possible to embed the system $\RNr{}$ of real numbers in the system of matrices, for example by turning a number $x$ into the matrix $[x]$ consisting of a single entry. Thus the system of all matrices vastly extends the system of real numbers, an extension which is enormously versatile and powerful.

                      We will use matrices for a variety of purposes: they will enable us to obtain additional tools to solve systems of linear equations. Further, we will learn how to use matrices to transform space, how to manipulate the location of objects in space, and how alter the shape of objects in space.

                      RREF = Row Reduced Echelon Form

                      We are now going to learn how to work with systems of linear equations more economically. Observe that, when reducing a system of linear equations to RREF , we really only manipulate its coefficients, not its variables. So, by carrying the variables along all the time we just do a lot of unnecessary work. – How can we do better?

                      We save ourselves a lot of work by extracting from the system its coefficients and writing them in a rectangle whose rows and columns resemble the original system.                       $$ \begin{array}{l} \left( \dfrac{9}{2}\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{5}{2}\, z , \dfrac{1}{2}\, +\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, y\, -\, \dfrac{1}{2}\, z , y , z \right) = \\ \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot (9,1,0,0) + y\cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},1,0) + z\cdot (-\tfrac{5}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},0,1) \end{array} $$

                        We use the term matrix to refer to any rectangular arrangement of objects.                             Determinants

                            Introduction

                            The determinant is a function which receives as input a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$ and yields, as output, a number denoted by $\det(\Mtrx{A})$. We are interested in determinants because:

                            1. determinants enable us to develop the concept of `oriented volume' in $n$-dimensional space, and

                            2. determinants provide a new test for invertibility of a square matrix $\Mtrx{A}$: $\Mtrx{A}$ is invertible exactly when $\det(\Mtrx{A})\neq 0$.

                            Let us preview our determinant topics in some more detail.

                            A preview of how to compute determinants

                            Preview: Computing Determinants

                            To define a quantity $Q$ depending on a number $n$ of inputs recursively means:

                            1. First define $Q$ for a single input.

                            2. Using this information, define $Q$ for two inputs.

                            3. Using knowledge of $Q$ for two inputs, define $Q$ for three inputs.

                            4. etc.

                            Determinants of square matrices are defined recursively as follows:

                            The determinant of a $(1,1)$-matrix $\Mtrx{A} = [x]$ is

                            $$\det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det [x] := x$$

                            The determinant of a $(2,2)$-matrix

                            $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \end{array} \right] $$

                            is given by

                            $$ \det(\Mtrx{A}) = \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 \end{array} \right] := {\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 $$

                            The recursive nature of this definition becomes more explicit if we write

                            $${\color{red} x_1}y_2 - {\color{red} x_2}y_1 = {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det[ y_2 ] - {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det [y_1]$$

                            The determinant of a $(3,3)$-matrix

                            $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] $$

                            is given by

                            $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &= \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} {\color{red} x_1} & y_1 & z_1 \\ {\color{red} x_2} & y_2 & z_2 \\ {\color{red} x_3} & y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right] \\ % &:= {\color{red} x_1}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_2 & z_2 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ -\ {\color{red} x_2}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_3 & z_3 \end{array} \right]\ +\ {\color{red} x_3}\cdot \det\left[ \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_2 & z_2 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

                            Now suppose the determinant of matrices of size $(n,n)$ has been defined. Then the determinant of an $(n+1,n+1)$-matrix

                            $$ A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} {\color{red} a_{1,1}} & a_{1,2} & \cdots & a_{1,n+1} \\ {\color{red} a_{2,1}} & a_{2,2} & \cdots & a_{2,n+1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}} & a_{n+1,2} & \cdots & a_{n+1,n+1} \\ \end{array} \right] $$

                            is given by

                            $$ \aligned \det(\Mtrx{A}) &:= {\color{red} a_{1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{1,1}) - {\color{red} a_{2,1}}\cdot \det(A_{2,1}) + - \cdots \\ &\qquad \cdots + (-1)^{(n+1)+1} {\color{red} a_{n+1,1}}\cdot \det(A_{n+1,1}) \endaligned $$

                            In this formula $A_{i,1}$ is the $(n,n)$-matrix which results from $\Mtrx{A}$ by removing the $i$-th row and the first column.

                            As you can see, this recursive formula for computing a determinant is not difficult in principle. However, the number of computational steps involved increases rapidly with the size of $\Mtrx{A}$.

                            A preview of how to compute with determinants

                            Preview: Rules for Computing with Determinants

                            The following rules for computing with determinants lead to an interpretation of determinants as `oriented volume' and, in addition, can often simplify the process of evaluating a determinant

                            Multilinear When applying the determinant operation to $(n,n)$-matrices, varying one column vector, while keeping the others fixed, yields a linear function $\RNr{n} \to \RNr{}$. For example:

                            $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} + {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} + {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} + {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 6} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 9} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ &=\ {\color{red} 3}\cdot \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{red} 1} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 2} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{red} 3} & 1 \end{array} \right]\ +\ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & {\color{green} a} & 7 \\ 1 & {\color{green} b} & 2 \\ 1 & {\color{green} c} & 1 \end{array} \right] \endaligned $$

                            Alternating   Interchanging any two columns of a matrix reverses the sign of its determinant. For example:

                            $$ \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = -\det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] = 1 $$

                            Additional rules

                            • $\det(\Mtrx{A}^T) = \det(\Mtrx{A})$

                            • $\det(\Mtrx{A}\cdot \Mtrx{B}) = \det(\Mtrx{A}) \det(\Mtrx{B})$

                            A preview of ‘orientation’

                            Preview: Orientation

                            We introduce the concept of orientation of $\RNr{n}$ as a means for distinguishing an object in $\RNr{n}$ from its mirrored image. In this preview we only discuss the visualizable dimensions 1,2,3.

                            1-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{}$ is given by a nonzero vector $\Vect{x} = (x)$. We say

                            $\Vect{x}$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{}$

                            if $ x> 0 $

                            $\omega(\Vect{x}) = +1$

                            $\Vect{x}$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{}$

                            if $ x < 0 $

                            $\omega(\Vect{x}) = -1$

                            `noncolinear' means: don't lie on one line; in other words: point in distinct directions

                            2-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{2}$ is given by a pair of noncolinear vectors $(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$. We say

                            $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{2}$

                            if the lesser of the two angles from $\Vect{x}$ to $\Vect{y}$ occurs in the counterclockwise direction

                            $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} ) = +1$

                            $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{2}$

                            if the lesser of the two angles from $\Vect{x}$ to $\Vect{y}$ occurs in the clockwise direction

                            $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} ) = -1$

                            `noncoplanar' means: don't lie in one plane

                            3-dimensional orientation – An orientation of $\RNr{3}$ is given by a triple of noncoplanar vectors $(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$. We say

                            $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive orientation of $\RNr{3}$

                            if it is possible to align your right hand's thumb with $\Vect{x}$, its index finger with $\Vect{y}$, and its middle finger with $\Vect{z}$.

                            without harming your hand

                            $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} ) = +1$

                            $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the negative orientation of $\RNr{3}$

                            if it is possible to align your left hand's thumb with $\Vect{x}$, its index finger with $\Vect{y}$, and its middle finger with $\Vect{z}$.

                            without harming your hand

                            $\omega(\Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} ) = -1$

                            A preview of ‘oriented volume’

                            Preview: Oriented Volume

                            The central result of this section is the following geometrical interpretation of the determinant operation.

                            Theorem – Let $\Vect{x}_1, \dots , \Vect{x}_n$ be $n$ vectors in $\RNr{n}$, and write them as column vectors. Then

                            $$\det [ \Vect{x}_1\ \dots\ \Vect{x}_n] = \omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n ) \cdot \Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$$

                            In the formula above, $\omega( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the orientation of $\RNr{n}$ represented by the $n$-tuple of vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$. This is $+1$ if the orientation is positive, and it is $-1$ if the orientation is negative. Finally, $\Vol( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$ denotes the volume of the slanted box in $\RNr{n}$ spanned by the vectors $( \Vect{x}_1 , \dots , \Vect{x}_n )$.

                            Already the following two examples illustrate the simple beauty of this result.

                            Example – the result

                            $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rr} 4320 & 4310 \\ 89 & 87 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 4320\cdot 87\ -\ 4310\cdot 89 \\ &=\ 375840\ -\ 383590 \\ &<\ 0 \endaligned $$

                            means

                            • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (4320,89)$ and $\Vect{y} = (4310,87)$ span a parallelogram whose area is $\Abs{375840 - 383590} = 383590 - 375840$.

                            • The pair of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$ represents the negative or clockwise orientation of the plane.

                            Example – the result

                            $$ \aligned \det \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 3 & 7 & -1 \\ -2 & 4 & 3 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{array} \right]\ &=\ 72 + 21 - (-4 + (-84)) \\ &=\ 181 > 0 \endaligned $$

                            means

                            • The vectors $\Vect{x} = (3,-2,1)$, $\Vect{y} = (7,4,0)$, $\Vect{z} = (-1,3,6)$ span a slanted box in $\RNr{3}$ whose volume is $181$.

                            • The ordered triple of vectors $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \Vect{z} )$ represents the positive or right hand orientation of the $\RNr{3}$.

                            A preview of ‘cross product’

                            Preview: Cross Product

                            The cross product of two vectors $\Vect{x} = (x_1,x_2,x_3)$ and $\Vect{y} = (y_1,y_2,y_3)$ in $\RNr{3}$ is the vector in $\RNr{3}$ given by the formula

                            $$ \aligned \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }\ &=\ \left( \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_2 & y_2 \\ x_3 & y_3 \end{array} \right] , -\left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_3 & y_3 \end{array} \right] , \det \left[ \begin{array}{cc} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \end{array} \right] \right) \\ % &=\ \left( x_2y_3-x_3y_2 , -(x_1y_3-x_3y_1) , x_1y_2-x_2y_1 \right) \endaligned $$

                            Below we formulate the algebraic properties and the geometric properties of the cross product, as well as its relationship to the dot product and the determinant.

                            For deeper reasons, the cross product of two vectors is only available in $\RNr{3}$ and in $\RNr{7}$.

                            Algebraic properties of the cross product

                            • bilinear

                              $\CrssPr{ (\Vect{x} + \Vect{x}' )}{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x}' }{ \Vect{y} }$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ (\Vect{y} + \Vect{y}') }$ $=$ $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } + \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y}' }$ $\CrssPr{ (t\cdot \Vect{x}) }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $t\cdot ( \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } )$

                            • antisymmetric

                              $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ $=$ $-\left( \CrssPr{ \Vect{y} }{ \Vect{x} } \right)$

                            Geometric properties of the cross product

                            • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ is perpendicular to both $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

                            • If $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } \neq \Vect{0}$, then the vector triple $( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} , \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })$ represents the right hand orientation of $\RNr{3}$.

                            • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } = \Vect{0}$ if and only if $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ are parallel.

                            • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$.

                            • If $\Vect{x},\Vect{y} \neq \Vect{0}$ then $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } } = \abs{ \Vect{x} } \abs{ \Vect{y} }\cdot \sin \sphericalangle( \Vect{x} , \Vect{y} )$.

                            • $\abs{ \CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} } }^2 = \abs{ \Vect{x} }^2 \abs{ \Vect{y} }^2 - ( \DotPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} })^2$

                            • $\CrssPr{ \Vect{a} }{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) } = (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{y} } )\cdot \Vect{x}\ -\ (\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ \Vect{x} }) \cdot \Vect{y}$.

                            Cross product, dot product and determinant

                            $$\DotPr{ \Vect{a} }{ (\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }) } = \det[ \Vect{a}\ \ \Vect{x} \ \ \Vect{y} ]$$

                            1. How to compute determinants   We first define the determinant of $(1,1)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(2,2)$-matrices, then the determinants of $(3,3)$-matrices and so on. – A few experiments will show you that, with increasing size of matrices, the number of computations one has to carry out increases extremely rapidly.

                            2. Rules for computing with determinants – Given that computing determinants of large matrices requires a lot of computations, it is doubly important that there are rules for computing with determinants which are quick and efficient. Some of these rules may seem counter intuitive at first. However, we will explain how these rules correspond perfectly to the properties of the volume function on boxes.

                              In addition, these rules will guide us to an alternate method of computing determinants using row and column operations. For determinants of large matrices this method is much more efficient than the recursive one we discussed earlier.

                            3. Orientation of space – Each $\RNr{n}$ carries two distinct orientations. This is a subtle property of space. In $\RNr{3}$ we encounter it by considering our two hands. While our hands are mirrored images of one another, they are not interchangeable, that is: we cannot replace a left hand by a right hand. Thus we need to distinguish carefully between ‘left’ and ‘right’. We encounter the need for such a distinction in each $\RNr{n}$, and the determinant operation will help us make such a distinction.

                            4. Oriented volume – Here we combine what we learned about properties of the determinant operation with properties of the volume of boxes and orientation. The result is the surprisingly powerful notion of oriented volume, computed using determinants.

                            5. Cross product – operation is only defined for pairs of vectors in $\RNr{3}$. Given two vectors $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$ from $\RNr{3}$, it returns the vector $\CrssPr{ \Vect{x} }{ \Vect{y} }$ of $\RNr{3}$, called the cross product of $\Vect{x}$ and $\Vect{y}$. The geometric properties of the cross product operation make it a very powerful tool in mechanical engineering and in parts of physics.

                            6. Cramer's rule – if a system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ variables has an invertible coefficient matrix, then we can express the unique solution of this system by a formula which uses the determinant operation.

                            Some historic highlights

                            • The history of determinants started with an analysis of the number of solutions of a system of linear equations. The determinant of the unaugmented coefficient matrix of a homogeneous system of $n$ linear equations is nonzero exactly when the system has the unique solution $(0,\dots ,0)$.

                            • The term ‘determinant’ appeared first in C. F. Gauß’s work on quadratic forms to express the fact that the determinant determines the properties of the quadratic form. However, the concept to which Gauß applied the term ‘determinant’ is distinct from the concept which we call ‘determinant’ today.

                            • The current use of the term ‘determinant’ goes back at least as far as A.-L. Cauchy . He is credited with publishing the most complete of the early works on determinants in 1812 .

                            • Later Weierstrass used an axiomatic definition of ‘determinant’. This means he used properties like ‘multilinear’, ‘alternating’, to define the determinant.
